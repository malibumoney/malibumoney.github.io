1.     Radiation, Temperature, Energy Balance

a.     What is the difference between temperature and heat?

                                               i.     Temperature is the average kinetic motion of molecules in a body. We measure temperature with a thermometer. Scales: Celsius, Fahrenheit, Kelvin

                                             ii.     Heat is energy transferred because temperature difference. Heat entering object is stored as internal energy, it is both kinetic and potential. Heat can also cause objects to change phase, which increases potential energy.

b.     Define and give examples (for the atmosphere) of the heat transfer mechanisms

                                               i.     Sensible heat: most familiar form; quality can be sensed by touch, and measured with a thermometer; transferred by conduction or convection.

                                             ii.     Latent heat: “hidden heat”; thermometer cannot measure; molecular motion involved in changes of state (phase)

                                           iii.     Latent heat transfer – the transfer of heat from an evaporating surface to the atmosphere

                                            iv.     Radiation: solar energy travels through vacuum of space

c.     Define latent heat, and explain why it is important in this class

                                               i.     Latent heat is the hidden heat that a thermometer cannot measure, the molecular motion involved in changes of state

3.     Weather Systems I

a.     What drives all wind?

                                               i.     Origination of wind

1.     Uneven heating of Earth’s surface creates temperature and pressure gradients

2.     Direction of wind results from pressure gradients

3.     Winds blow form high pressure to low pressure

b.     How do we name winds?

                                               i.     A wind is always named according to the direction from which it blows. For example, a wind blowing from west to east is a west wind.

c.     Why do we have atmospheric pressure? How do we measure it? Map it?

                                               i.     Atmospheric pressure is the result of gravity exerting its force on air in atmosphere.

                                             ii.     We measure in lbs per square inch, we use a barometer

                                           iii.     We map pressure with isobars           

1.     Typical units are millibars or inches of mercury

                                            iv.     Factors influencing atmospheric pressure

1.     Density – at higher density, particles are closer together and collide more frequently, increasing pressure

2.     Temperature – warmer particles move faster and collide more frequently, increasing pressure

d.     What 3 factors influence atmospheric pressure?

                                               i.     Humidity – as humidity increases air pressure decreases

                                             ii.     Altitude – as altitude increases pressure decreases

                                           iii.     Temperature – as temperature increases air pressure decreases

e.     What is an adiabatic process and how does it work?

                                               i.     An adiabatic process is a thermodynamic process where a fluid becomes warmer or cooler without getting heat from, or giving it to, something else. Usually the temperature instead changes because of changes in pressure.

f.      Explain all of the forces that govern the wind, and how they work

                                               i.     Pressure gradient force

1.     Characterized by wind moving from high to low pressure, always

2.     Winds blow at right angles to isobars

                                             ii.     Coriolis force: resulting from our rotation

1.     Turns wind to the right in the Northern Hemisphere, left in the Southern Hemisphere

2.     Only affects wind direction, not speed, though faster winds turn more

                                           iii.     Friction

1.     Wind is slowed by Earth’s surface due to friction, does not affect upper levels

g.     What is the Coriolis force? How does it work, and vary by latitude?

                                               i.     As air moves from high to low pressure in the northern hemisphere, it is deflected to the right by the Coriolis force. In the southern hemisphere, air moving from high to low pressure is deflected to the left by the Coriolis force. Strongest at equator.
